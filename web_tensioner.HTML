Single Speed Chain Tensioner

What I Did:

I designed and built a custom chain tensioner to convert my non-single-speed bike into a single-speed bike. The bike frame was not originally designed for a single gear, and being an older model, off-the-shelf tensioners didn’t fit. My solution included a wheel distance adjustment mechanism and a spring-loaded tensioner to maintain proper chain slack.

How I Did It:

I measured the bike’s mounting points using a bevel gauge and reference photos, then modeled an approximate geometry in CAD. Since the frame consisted of non-standard bent and welded steel tubes, I used 3D-printed fit tests to refine the design. I modeled the tensioner in Creo Parametric, following proper CAD practices to allow geometry updates without breaking the file. I repurposed steel parts from an old derailleur, cutting and drilling them to form the tensioner arm and jockey pulley mount, using printed stencils to guide fabrication.

Results:

The tensioner functions reliably. The spring applies strong chain tension, and the design accommodates different single-speed gear sizes without manual adjustment.

Key Features

One Bolt Clamp Design

I initially tried a simple two-bolt design, but found it bulky and difficult to install. Inspired by the dustbin latching mechanism from a broken Roomba I was salvaging, I applied the same principle to create a more compact and user-friendly clamp design.

Repurposed Aluminum Arm

I repurposed an aluminum arm from a broken derailleur, cutting it to length and reusing the original spring and clip. I adjusted the spring hole angles to achieve the correct chain tension, and carefully designed tolerances so the arm could move freely once the C-clip was installed. The arm’s position was optimized in Creo using a kinematic mechanism simulation to approximate the ideal placement and tension for the desired chain length.
